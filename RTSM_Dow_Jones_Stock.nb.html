ADF Test for Stationarity

Objective:

To check whether the stock return data and closing price data are stationary or not

Analysis:

The Augmented Dickey-Fuller Test is a statistical test used to determine whether a given time series data is stationary or not. Stationarity of time series data indicates that the mean of the series do not change over time, making it easier to model and forecast.

H0: Time Series is not stationary H1: Times Series is stationary

Here p-value for closing-price:0.9758 > 0.05, indicating series not stationary

Whereas, p-values for return series is: 0.01 < 0.05 hence series is stationary

Results:

p-values for TATA power returns series is: 0.01 < 0.05 hence TATA_return series is stationary.

Managerial Implication:

With stationary data, modeling and forecasting TATA stock returns become more reliable as the statistical properties of the data remain consistent over time.

ACF and PACF Plot

Objective: ACF: p and PACF: q

To assess the presence of autocorrelation in a time series data set(correlation between a time series and its lagged versions). In other words, it indicates whether the past values of a series can be used to predict its future values

Analysis:

  1. The ACF plots for the series Stock_Return shows the autocorrelation coefficients for different lags. The autocorrelation coefficient at lag k measures the linear dependence between the series and its lagged version by k periods. In this specific plot, the autocorrelation coefficients are plotted on the y-axis, and the lags are plotted on the x-axis.
  2. The PACF plot specifically focuses on identifying the lags at which the series exhibits significant autocorrelations, net of the correlations at shorter lags.

Results:

  1. From the ACF plot, most of the autocorrelation coefficients fall within the shaded area around zero. This suggests that there is slightly statistically significant autocorrelation present in the series at any of the lags depicted.
  2. In other words, the past values of the stock returns slightly appear to have a significant predictive power for future returns.
  3. There might be a significant partial autocorrelation at a specific lag. This suggests that the series exhibits an autocorrelation at that lag, which is not explained by the correlations at shorter lags.

Managerial Implication:

The presence of a significant partial autocorrelation in stock returns suggests that past values of the stock returns might have a predictive power for future returns

Auto-Correlation test for residuals in ARIMA Modelling

Objective:

To find out whether there is auto-correlation between residuals of ARIMA model and hence define validity of using ARIMA Forecast

Analysis:

  1. The Box-Pierce test is used to assess the goodness-of-fit of a time series model by examining the autocorrelation of its residuals.
  2. In this analysis, the test was applied to the residuals of an ARMA model, specifically to evaluate whether there is significant autocorrelation present.

Null Hypothesis (H0): There is no autocorrelation in the residuals of ARIMA Model Alternate Hypothesis (H1): There is autocorrelation in the residuals of ARIMA Model

Here p-value for ARIMA Model residuals is :0.874 > 0.05, indicating residuals have no auto-correlation

Results:

The obtained p-value of 0.874 is substantially greater than the conventional significance level of 0.05. Therefore, there we do not reject the null hypothesis and there is no autocorrelation in the residuals.

Managerial Implication:

The Box-Pierce test results provide reassurance regarding the adequacy of the ARMA model in forecasting the data, hence we can forecast the data with ARIMA Modelling

ARCH-LM test for heteroskedasticity

Objective:

To predict the models volatility based on ARCH-LM test for heteroskedasticity in residual-square of ARIMA model

Analysis:

  1. The ARCH (Autoregressive Conditional Heteroscedasticity) LM-test is used to assess whether there are ARCH effects present in the residuals of a time series model.
  2. ARCH effects refer to the presence of conditional heteroscedasticity, where the variance of the residuals depends on past values of the series.

Null Hypothesis (H0): There is no ARCH effects(Homoskedastic) Alternate Hypothesis (H1): There is ARCH effects present in residual-square(variance) (Heteroskedastic)

Here p-value for ARIMA Model residuals-square is : 2.2e-16 < 0.05, indicating heteroskedasticity in residual-square

Results:

The obtained p-value of 2.2e-16 is substantially less than the conventional significance level of 0.05. Therefore, we reject the null hypothesis and there is heteroskedasticity in residual-square.

Managerial Implication:

The significant ARCH effects detected in the squared residuals indicate that the current time series model fails to account for conditional heteroscedasticity adequately.
